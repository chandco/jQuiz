Last month, we met up with history buff Charles Graham for a walking tour of the historic ‘Square Mile’, the City of London. We had a fantastic time, learning some of the hidden secrets and amazing stories that make London the greatest city in the world. We wanted to share some of the things we learned with you, but we knew that no-one could put it quite as well as Charles himself. So we’ve asked him to put together a quiz to test your knowledge. Over to Charles, and his good friend Doctor Johnson…

"Why, Sir, you find no man, at all intellectual, who is willing to leave London. No, Sir, when a man is tired of London, he is tired of life; for there is in London all that life can afford."

So said Doctor Johnson in 1777, and yet who among us can really say they know the City of London?
Far too few people have been to the Tower of London, taken there by their parents when they were little or not at all, when if they went to Madrid or Paris, they would instantly go and see the equivalent landmarks there. The problem is that London residents often just take their city for granted. After all, it’s always there.
But how best to see the sights when people have so many ways to get around? From the London underground, the Croydon trams, by car, by bus, by train, by motorbike, or even by Boris bike. I even think there’s a helipad somewhere in Battersea.  Historically, there were also Hackney Carriages, the horse drawn omnibus, or even the Sedan Chair that was so popular in the 17th to early 19th century. Indeed, one of the most popular means historically has recently made a comeback. When there was only one bridge, you could easily hail a boat from the north bank of the Thames. If you wanted to go upstream you said: “Westward Ho!” If you wanted to go downstream you said: “Eastward Ho!” If you want to go to the south bank you said: “Oars!” As many people visiting the South Bank in the 16th and 17th century were going to visit ladies of dubious repute, it’s possible that there may be  a link between the words “Oars” and, well, “Whores”.

Perhaps the best way to get to see the city is foot. So, with that in mind…

Which of these famous London viewpoints is the highest?

  • St Paul’s Cathedral
  • The London Eye

  • Westminster Cathedral

At 135 metres high, the London Eye just pips St Paul’s to the tallest of these three viewpoints, although at 100 metres it’s not exactly short stuff itself. Westminster Cathedral (which is not to be confused with the Abbey!) comes in at 87 metres, and has lift access for those whose legs aren’t as strong. Interestingly, if you’re a British citizen and apply to your MP you can also see the view from Big Ben, at 97 metres high. Other good natural viewpoints in and around south London include Greenwich and Shooter Hill to the south east, Crystal Palace and one tree hill in the south , and Henry VIII’s Mound in Richmond Park. Parliament Hill, Primrose Hill, Harrow on the Hill and Hampstead Heath are worth a visit to the north. A lost London viewpoint is the city’s former tallest building. At 171 metres the BT Tower featured a revolving restaurant and cocktail bar, but was closed in 1980.

How are London Bridge, Southwark Bridge, Blackfriars Bridge, Millennium Bridge and Tower Bridge paid for and maintained?

  • From the Bridge House Estates
  • From a bequest Dick Wittington, thrice Mayor of London
  • From fixed levy charges on the City of London businesses.
The Bridge House Estates consists of land left in wills by various city merchants for the maintenance of old London Bridge which was built in 1201. The bridge was vital for London prosperity, so merchants would leave their money first to god and second to the bridge. The last such bequest was in 1675. Today the fund has a 300 million pound surplus after paying for, maintaining and building all the bridges and gives 15 million pounds to charity each year.

Which killed the most people in London?

  • London Blitz
  • The Great Plague
  • Boudicca’s Sack of London
The Great Plague of 1665-1666, the last major epidemic of bubonic plague in the Kingdom of England, killed approximately 100,000 people, which at the time was 15% of the population. In the early 1940s, the London Blitz caused widespread devastation and as many as 43,000 deaths. According to Tacitus, Boudicca’s Sack of the city killed 70,000 people, although modern historians believe the number to be closer to 20,000. Surprisingly, the Great Fire of London is only believed to have caused around 10 fatalities.

Which of these was not a medieval gate into the City of London?

Queen’s Gate in Kensington was never a medieval gate into the City of London. Ludgate Hill was one the three ancient hills in the city, the other two being Tower Hill and Cornhill. Which of these ships would you find in London?

  • HMS Victory
  • HMS Cavalier
  • The Cutty Sark
The famous tea clipper, the Cutty Sark, can be found in Greenwich and is a landmark on the route of the London Marathon (not to mention one of our venues!) HMS Victory, Nelson’s flagship, can be found in Portsmouth, while HMS Cavalier, a WWII destroyer, can be found in Chatham Historic Dockyard in Kent. Another famous ship in London is the Golden Hinde, a reconstruction of the flagship of Sir Francis Drake, the Tudor explorer.
